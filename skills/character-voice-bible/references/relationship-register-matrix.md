# Relationship Register Matrix

Use this file when the main risk is Korean hierarchy, intimacy, or address drift.

## What To Lock Per Pair

For each important character pair, define:

- address term
- speech level baseline
- when politeness rises
- when politeness drops
- interruption tolerance
- what counts as disrespect
- whether teasing is allowed
- whether direct emotional language is possible

## Common Korean Register Levers

Track the actual levers that move on the page:

- sentence ending family: `-습니다`, `-아요/어요`, plain form, clipped half-sentences
- address form: name, title, kinship term, omission, or second-person avoidance
- apology and deference markers: how often the speaker softens, hedges, or self-lowers
- permission to interrupt, joke, or leave meaning implicit

Shift only the levers supported by the relationship state. Do not drop from formal speech to intimate speech in one jump unless the scene gives a clear trigger.

## Common Drift Warnings

- Confession scenes often drop politeness too early.
- Rival pairs often sound too symmetrical unless one side controls status differently.
- Family pairs may keep emotional distance even with informal endings.
- Subordinates do not automatically sound submissive; status handling and emotional boldness are separate.
- Romance progression should affect address and hesitation patterns, not only softness.
- Speakers often avoid direct second-person pronouns in Korean; forcing explicit "you" can flatten status texture.
- A softer ending does not automatically mean intimacy; it may signal caution, appeasement, or strategic de-escalation.
