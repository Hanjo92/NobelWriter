# Longform Workflow

Use this file when the user needs the full planning order for a long novel or series.

## Planning Order

1. Lock the ending promise and series-scale promise.
2. Stabilize the story engine.
3. Choose the growth model for the long run.
4. Define world pressure and non-negotiable rules.
5. Build the core cast and relationship tensions.
6. Create the history spine that shapes the present.
7. Split the long work into volumes, arcs, or seasons.
8. Break each major arc into chapter-range objectives.
9. Create continuity, knowledge, and payoff trackers.
10. Hand off only the active subset to drafting.
11. After each drafting batch, update the trackers.

## Greenfield Default

If the user starts from a premise, hook, or synopsis rather than existing chapters, do this before any continuity work:

- build the story engine sheet
- choose a series expansion model
- create a series brief
- map the first volume or first major arc

Continuity documents come later unless the user already has drafted material.

## Lock The Ending Promise First

Decide what kind of fulfillment the long work is aiming at:

- revenge completed or transformed
- romance union, rupture, or impossible compromise
- succession, conquest, or regime change
- truth exposed or permanently buried
- ascent to power with price
- survival with identity cost

The exact ending can stay open, but the category of promise should be visible early because it shapes escalation and reader expectation.

## Frame The Series Scale

Lock early:

- target format: long novel, saga, serialized web novel, trilogy, etc.
- planned scale: approximate chapter count, volume count, or arc count
- genre and audience
- growth model: static cast, expanding ensemble, factional expansion, power progression, romance escalation, mystery layering
- drafting mode: preplanned, rolling outline, or hybrid

Choose only one primary growth model at first. Secondary growth can exist, but too many equal engines make the long run shapeless.

## Stabilize The Story Engine

Before building large documents, confirm:

- protagonist desire can sustain many chapters
- opposition can evolve rather than repeat
- stakes can escalate in more than one lane
- the world generates recurring pressure, not just one premise

If this layer is weak, fix it first. Longform planning built on a weak engine becomes decorative clutter.

## Choose The Growth Model

Pick the main way the project expands:

- power progression
- revenge deepening
- mystery layering
- romance escalation
- political faction spread
- expedition or travel accumulation
- family or generational widening

Match the volume structure to that expansion model. A revenge story should not expand like a wandering travelogue unless the user wants hybrid friction.

## Build Planning Artifacts By Necessity

Use the minimum stable set:

- story engine sheet: always for greenfield work
- story bible: always
- core cast matrix: almost always
- series expansion map: when the story must scale past one contained arc
- history timeline: when backstory materially drives present conflict
- volume plan: when the project exceeds one arc
- continuity ledger: when chapters accumulate
- knowledge-state tracker: when secrets, mysteries, lies, or factional asymmetry matter
- payoff tracker: when setup and delayed return are central to reader satisfaction

## Update Rhythm

Refresh the planning layer:

- before opening a new major arc
- after introducing new factions, locations, or rules
- after reveals, deaths, betrayals, awakenings, confessions, or world-rule changes
- after every meaningful batch of drafted chapters

## Failure Signs

Intervene if you see:

- character goals resetting between arcs
- the setting acting differently for convenience
- information appearing before the character earned it
- romantic or political tension repeating the same beat
- promised threats or objects disappearing
- timeline math failing under travel, injury, training, or investigation
