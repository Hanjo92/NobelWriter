# Dialogue Tone Transforms

Use this reference when the user wants the same dialogue content expressed in a different tone without breaking character, context, or Korean naturalness.

## Core Rule

Keep these fixed unless the user explicitly asks to change them:

- the speaker's underlying intent
- the relationship status implied by the scene
- the factual meaning needed for the plot
- the emotional consequence of the line

Change these to achieve the requested tone:

- sentence length
- directness
- diction level
- speech ending
- amount of subtext
- degree of emotional exposure

Do not turn a believable line into a stylish but implausible line.
Do not preserve a line that still sounds like summarized meaning wearing Korean words.

## Transformation Workflow

1. Identify what the line is doing.
2. Identify how directly the speaker can afford to say it.
3. Check the current emotional temperature.
4. Adjust syntax, ending, and word choice to the target tone.
5. Re-read it against the previous and next lines.
6. Remove any trace that the line was built from abstract meaning blocks before it became speech.

Label the line's job first:

- refuse
- test
- flirt
- threaten
- apologize
- stall
- comfort
- provoke
- hide information

If the line's job changes, it is no longer a tone conversion. It is a rewrite.

## Meaning-First Failure Signs

Treat these as rewrite triggers:

- the line explains the speaker's position too completely
- the line sounds like a synopsis of feeling instead of an utterance
- every semantic component is visible in order with no compression or dodge
- the line feels easier to summarize than to imagine someone saying

When this happens, rebuild from:

- what the speaker wants right now
- what they avoid saying directly
- the pressure of the prior line
- the shortest plausible spoken version

## Tone Axes

Use these axes in combination instead of treating tone as one slider.

### Direct -> Indirect

- direct: clear statement, less hedging, faster conflict
- indirect: softened claim, more implication, more social maneuvering

### Cold -> Warm

- cold: fewer emotional markers, clipped response, distance
- warm: small softeners, concern markers, more relational attention

### Rough -> Polished

- rough: shorter, blunter, more spoken compression
- polished: cleaner syntax, controlled wording, restrained edge

### Dominant -> Equal -> Defensive

- dominant: sets frame, closes options, leads rhythm
- equal: trades response at similar force
- defensive: explains, deflects, minimizes, retreats

## Default Conversion Rules

### To make a line more natural

- cut explanation the listener already knows
- replace complete sentence logic with spoken compression
- let subtext carry part of the meaning
- allow partial answers if tension is present

### To make a line softer

- reduce accusation vocabulary
- add hesitation, indirect framing, or room for retreat
- shift from judgment to observation

### To make a line sharper

- shorten the line
- remove padding
- move the sting to the end

### To make a line more masculine-coded

Use carefully and only when the user asks for this market tendency.

- favor brevity and action focus
- reduce self-explanatory emotion labels
- let challenge, decision, or practicality sit in front

Do not flatten all emotion. Keep pressure visible through omission and stance.

### To make a line more feminine-coded

Use carefully and only when the user asks for this market tendency.

- foreground relational reading and emotional implication
- allow softer entry with sharper subtext underneath
- use response shape that tracks the other person's mood as well as facts

Do not turn every line into ornate sensitivity. Keep scene pressure active.

### To rebalance toward neutral

- remove overt market-signaling diction
- keep both plot intent and emotional legibility
- prefer broadly readable spoken Korean over niche voice markers

## Conversion Sets

Each set preserves the core intent while shifting delivery.

### Set 1: Refusal

Base intent:
- "I will not help you."

Blunt:
```text
"안 해."
```

Softer:
```text
"그건 내가 못 하겠어."
```

Cold:
```text
"그 일엔 관여하지 않겠습니다."
```

Warm but firm:
```text
"미안한데, 이번엔 못 도와줘."
```

Male-oriented commercial:
```text
"그건 내 선 아니야."
```

Female-oriented commercial:
```text
"미안해. 그 부탁까지 받아 주면, 내가 너무 무너질 것 같아."
```

Neutral:
```text
"그건 도와줄 수 없어."
```

### Set 2: Suspicion

## Genre-Grounded Examples

Use these examples when the user asks for the same intent to feel native to a specific genre rather than merely softer or sharper.

### Traditional Fantasy: Oath-Bound Refusal

Context:
- A knight refuses a prince's reckless order in public.

Base intent:
- "I cannot obey that order."

Flat:
```text
"그 명령은 따를 수 없습니다."
```

Genre-shaped:
```text
"그 명은 받들 수 없습니다, 전하. 오늘 여기서 꺾이면 성문도 같이 무너집니다."
```

Why it works:
- keeps refusal but adds fealty, public pressure, and battlefield consequence

### Martial Arts: Suspicion Inside Courtesy

Context:
- A disciple suspects another sect member is hiding something.

Base intent:
- "You are hiding the truth."

Flat:
```text
"당신은 진실을 숨기고 있군요."
```

Genre-shaped:
```text
"말을 아끼시는군요. 숨기신 게 없으면 시선부터 그렇게 돌리진 않으셨을 텐데."
```

Why it works:
- keeps tension under courtesy
- sounds like a murim confrontation instead of direct accusation

### Modern Fantasy: Practical Comfort

Context:
- A hunter comforts a teammate right before entering a dungeon again.

Base intent:
- "You do not have to bear this alone."

Flat:
```text
"너 혼자 책임질 필요 없어."
```

Genre-shaped:
```text
"혼자 책임지려 하지 마. 이번 브리핑은 내가 맡고, 안쪽은 같이 들어가."
```

Why it works:
- keeps comfort actionable
- fits a modern fantasy scene where reassurance often comes through role-sharing

### Fusion Fantasy: Threat Through Strategy

Context:
- A regressor warns a noble who is testing limits.

Base intent:
- "If you continue, I will crush you."

Flat:
```text
"계속하면 당신을 무너뜨리겠습니다."
```

Genre-shaped:
```text
"한 번 더 선 넘으시면, 이번엔 체면으로 안 끝납니다. 가문부터 계산서가 나갈 겁니다."
```

Why it works:
- preserves threat while making it political and strategic
- suits fusion fantasy's power-plus-structure texture

### Romance: Defensive Flirtation

Base intent:

Genre-shaped:


Why it works:
- mixes attraction with self-protective distance
- feels more usable in live romantic dialogue

### Traditional Fantasy Female-Oriented: Comfort With Status Pressure

## Final Check

After conversion, verify:

- the same speaker would still plausibly say it
- the surrounding characters would respond naturally to it
- the converted line still belongs to this scene, not just this meaning
- the new tone supports the chosen genre and audience rather than fighting it
