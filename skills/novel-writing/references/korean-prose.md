# Korean Prose Guidance

Use this reference when drafting or revising Korean fiction rather than generic fiction advice.

## 1. Default Language Policy

- Write the final output in Korean unless the user explicitly requests another language.
- Avoid sentence patterns that read like direct translation from English.
- Prefer idiomatic Korean word order and connective choices over literal phrasing.
- Avoid sentences that feel assembled from pre-decided meaning blocks rather than discovered in Korean phrasing.
- Do not force Sino-Korean words into awkward native-Korean replacements just to sound "more Korean."
- Prefer the vocabulary that real Korean speakers and genre readers would naturally expect, not merely the simplest or most purified wording.

## 1B. Lexical Naturalness Rule

`Natural Korean` does not mean `maximally native Korean vocabulary`.

Prefer the word Korean readers would actually expect in that register, even if it is a common Sino-Korean term.

Keep these defaults:

- use familiar, high-frequency Korean literary vocabulary
- keep common Sino-Korean nouns and verbs when they are the natural choice
- avoid rare, overly purified, or dictionary-like substitutes that draw attention to themselves
- avoid replacing a stable literary word with a clumsy paraphrase just because the original has Sino-Korean roots

If choosing between a forced native term and a common literary/Sino-Korean term, choose the one that sounds normal in live prose.

## 1C. Lexical Red Flags

Treat these as warning signs:

- the word is technically Korean but feels unfamiliar in fiction prose
- a common word was replaced by a longer paraphrase for no scene-level benefit
- the sentence becomes harder to read after lexical purification
- the diction sounds like a style guide exercise rather than a novel
- one odd lexical choice draws attention away from the scene

When this happens, restore the more ordinary and natural word.

## 1A. Meaning-First Trace Check

Watch for signs that the line was built by deciding the meaning first and only afterward wrapping it in Korean:

- the sentence feels like a verbal summary of intent rather than a line someone would actually say or narrate
- abstract meaning units appear in neat sequence without natural compression
- the wording explains what the sentence is doing instead of simply doing it
- the sentence can be paraphrased much more naturally in half the length
- the line reads like a polished explanation of emotion, motive, or tension rather than lived language

If these traces appear, rebuild the sentence from speaker pressure, image, reaction, or scene movement instead of editing the surface only.

## 2. Dialogue And Register

Before drafting dialogue, decide:

- whether narration is literary, commercial, or web-fiction leaning
- whether dialogue should be formal, casual, blunt, archaic, or mixed
- how each major relationship affects address terms and speech endings
- what each speaker wants from the exchange right now
- what each speaker is willing to say directly versus indirectly

Default to speech that sounds like a real utterance made under live pressure.
If the work requires genre distance, use the form of speech that sounds natural inside that genre rather than flattening everything into modern casual Korean.

Check for consistency in:

- honorific markers
- sentence endings
- titles and kinship terms
- name usage versus role usage

If conflict rises, dialogue may become shorter or harsher, but the change should feel motivated rather than random.

## 2A. Reality Check For Dialogue

Run this check before finalizing dialogue:

- Would a real person in this social position talk this directly?
- Does the line fit what the speaker already knows, or is it only informing the reader?
- Is the sentence short enough to sound spoken if the scene is tense?
- Does the reply actually answer, dodge, soften, escalate, or redirect the previous line?
- Could the meaning be carried with less explanation and more implication?
- Does the wording sound like live Korean conversation rather than written narration?
- Does the line reflect the immediate context, or could it be pasted into any other scene unchanged?

If a line fails this check, shorten it, remove explanation, or shift part of the meaning into subtext, gesture, or narration.

Also ask:

- Does this sound like the speaker found the words in the moment, or like the writer pre-summarized the meaning and assigned it to the speaker?
- Did I replace a normal spoken word with an unnatural "pure Korean" alternative that this speaker would never use?
- Does this line still sound speakable when read aloud, or only acceptable on the page?

## 2B. Default Dialogue Rules

Prefer:

- short, intention-driven lines
- incomplete answers when the relationship is strained
- indirect wording when social pressure matters
- repetition only when it reflects panic, hesitation, or fixation
- replies that carry over the previous line's mood, pressure, or avoidance
- sentence endings that sound spoken aloud rather than formally written
- distinction between speakers through rhythm, diction, reaction style, honorific handling, and emotional leakage

Avoid by default:

- full-sentence explanation of backstory in live conversation
- literary narration disguised as spoken words
- characters naming emotions they would normally hide
- every line sounding equally polished or equally clever
- modern casual diction in scenes that require formal distance
- replies that restate context both speakers already share
- dialogue that ignores interruption, hesitation, or evasiveness in tense moments
- dialogue where every speaker sounds equally trimmed, equally clever, or equally explanatory

## 2C. Contextual Colloquial Dialogue Rules

When writing spoken dialogue, apply these defaults unless the user requests a stylized voice:

- let the previous line shape wording, not just topic
- allow speakers to answer sideways, partially, or defensively
- shorten syntax when urgency or embarrassment rises
- use narration for precise explanation and let dialogue carry intent, pressure, and relationship
- keep vocabulary appropriate to age, class, profession, and intimacy
- allow short lines, but do not cut them down until they read like headings, labels, or memo fragments
- prefer the length a person could naturally blurt out over meaning-only shorthand

If the line feels too neat, too explanatory, or too complete, rewrite it in a form that sounds easier to say than to read.
If the line feels like it was built from meaning blocks such as emotion -> reason -> conclusion, rebuild it from pressure and response instead.
Do not mechanically ban endings such as `-지`, `-군`, or `-나`; instead, check whether the overall speech texture is too literary or archaic for that character and moment.

## 2D. Transformation Examples

Use these as pattern references, not fixed templates.

### Example 1: Shared Context, No Reader Explanation

Context:
- Two siblings already know their father missed another promise.

Awkward:
```text
"아버지는 항상 약속을 지키지 않으셨고, 이번에도 우리를 실망하게 만들었어."
```

Natural:
```text
"또 안 왔네."
"그러게. 기대한 네가 바보다."
```

Why it works:
- removes explanation both speakers already know
- lets subtext carry history and resentment

### Example 2: Tense Apology

Context:
- A junior employee is late and the manager is angry, but others are listening.

Awkward:
```text
"팀장님, 제가 지각한 이유는 지하철이 예상보다 늦게 도착했기 때문입니다."
```

Natural:
```text
"죄송합니다. 지하철이 멈춰서 늦었습니다."
"다음엔 출발부터 다르게 하세요."
```

Why it works:
- sounds spoken instead of report-like
- keeps the manager's reply socially controlled in public

### Example 3: Romantic Distance

Context:
- Two people are hurt after a fight and neither wants to apologize first.

Awkward:
```text
"나는 네가 어제 했던 말 때문에 매우 상처를 받았고 그래서 지금 화가 나 있어."
```

Natural:
```text
"어제 그 말, 너무했어."
"알아. 근데 나도 할 말 없었던 건 아니야."
```

Why it works:
- keeps emotion visible without over-explaining it
- leaves room for defensiveness and unresolved tension

### Example 4: Parent And Teenager

Context:
- A mother catches her son sneaking in late at night.

Awkward:
```text
"너는 지금 시간이 몇 시인지 알고 있니? 나는 네 안전에 대해서 매우 걱정했다."
```

Natural:
```text
"지금 몇 시인 줄 알아?"
"엄마, 그만 좀."
"그만은 네가 했어야지."
```

Why it works:
- reflects familiar family rhythm
- uses interruption and pushback natural to the relationship

### Example 5: Suspicion Without Explicit Accusation

Context:
- A detective suspects a witness is lying but cannot accuse them yet.

Awkward:
```text
"당신의 진술은 여러 측면에서 신뢰하기 어렵다고 판단됩니다."
```

Natural:
```text
"아까랑 말이 좀 다르네요."
"제가요?"
"시간만 다시 맞춰 보죠."
```

Why it works:
- preserves pressure without overt exposition
- makes the exchange tactical rather than declarative

## 3. Naming And Address

Track separately:

- legal or full name
- familiar name
- title or rank
- relationship-based address
- nickname or insult

Characters should not switch address forms without a social or emotional reason.

## 4. Narrative Texture

Prefer:

- concrete images tied to character perception
- short explanatory bridges between emotional beats
- paragraph breaks that preserve reading flow on Korean screens
- sentence flow that can be understood on first read
- verbs and sentence endings that land cleanly rather than trailing through abstraction

Use restraint with:

- stacked modifiers
- repetitive adverbs
- overuse of poetic abstraction
- excessive exposition before conflict appears
- long chains of perception phrases such as "느끼고 있었다", "생각이 들었다", "보이고 있었다" when a more direct sentence would work
- abstract nouns that can be replaced with action, image, or reaction

Let narration carry information that spoken dialogue would sound unnatural delivering.
Let lexical choice serve readability first. Do not purify vocabulary at the cost of flow.
Prefer sensory or reaction-based phrasing over unsupported abstraction, but do not ban abstraction when the scene, character, or genre genuinely needs it.

## 4B. POV-Specific Narration Control

Decide whether the scene uses `1인칭`, `3인칭 제한`, or a more distant third-person stance before revising narration.
By default, keep one POV owner per chapter. Treat POV switching as a special-case move allowed only at a chapter boundary unless the user explicitly asks for a different structure.

### First-Person

Use these defaults:

- keep perception, judgment, and uncertainty inside the narrator's lived awareness
- let diction, noticing habits, and sentence bias reflect the narrator's personality and situation
- allow self-interpretation, but do not let the line sound like an outside analyst describing the narrator
- keep self-observation plausible; the narrator can infer, notice, remember, or rationalize, but should not watch themself from impossible camera distance

Avoid:

- reporting another character's interior state as fact unless the narrator is inferring it
- sliding into neutral author summary that no longer sounds like this narrator
- overusing explicit self-diagnosis when a reaction, sensory shift, or thought fragment would feel truer

### Third-Person Limited

Use these defaults:

- anchor each paragraph to one perceiver unless the user explicitly wants a shift
- let description pass through that character's access, vocabulary range, and current pressure
- keep other characters readable through action, speech, and observable reaction
- use distance deliberately: close-limited can feel nearly interior, while a slightly farther camera can summarize more cleanly without changing owners

For close-limited third person:

- let the line stay near the focal character's immediate sensation, reaction, and active interpretation
- prefer wording that feels filtered through what this character would notice first under current pressure
- keep summary short; let the scene arrive through live contact with the moment

For slightly farther limited third person:

- keep ownership with the same focal character, but allow cleaner compression and more neutral phrasing
- summarize short stretches of reaction or situation when that improves flow
- do not slip so far outward that the prose starts sounding omniscient or ownerless

Avoid:

- unexplained mind-reading of non-POV characters
- sentence openings that sound camera-neutral and then suddenly borrow one character's private thought
- floating information that no one in the current scene could notice yet

### Distant Third-Person

Use this only when the user clearly wants a broader, more authorial stance.

Keep these rules:

- signal the distance early through consistent sentence texture
- do not oscillate line by line between intimate interior access and detached summary
- keep the governing intelligence stable even when the prose is more panoramic

If the user does not explicitly ask for omniscient narration, prefer `3인칭 제한`.

## 4C. POV Drift Check

Run this check on narration before finalizing:

- In `1인칭`, does the line still sound like something this narrator could think, recall, or frame?
- In `1인칭`, did the prose slip into author commentary that knows more than the narrator can know right now?
- In `3인칭 제한`, is the perceiver still obvious?
- In `3인칭 제한`, did the narration quietly step into another character's interior state instead of keeping that POV change for a chapter boundary?
- Did the prose attempt a POV switch inside the same chapter instead of saving that change for a chapter boundary?
- Does the narration distance stay consistent across the paragraph, or does it jump from intimate to detached with no reason?

If POV drifts, fix ownership first, then rhythm and style.
If you need concrete side-by-side models, read [pov-narration-examples.md](pov-narration-examples.md).

## 4A. Narration Readability Check

Run this check on narration before finalizing:

- Does the sentence read smoothly on the first pass?
- Is the subject clear without backtracking?
- Are too many modifiers stacked before the core image lands?
- Would the line improve if one abstract emotion were shown through reaction instead?
- Is the sentence long because the thought is complex, or only because the syntax is loose?

If a sentence catches in the mouth or eye, simplify the syntax before adding style back in.
If a sentence sounds like it was first drafted as an outline note and then expanded into prose, collapse it back to the core image and rewrite from there.
If one word feels conspicuously purified or unusual, replace it with the more ordinary literary word before changing larger structure.

## 5. Web Novel Adjustment

If the user is writing web fiction:

- front-load hook and tension
- keep scene turns legible
- end chapters with a reveal, decision, danger, or emotional snap
- favor high readability over ornate density

## 6. Revision Checklist

Check for:

- unnatural imported phrasing
- inconsistent speech level
- exposition blocks that delay scene motion
- repeated emotional wording
- unclear subject reference in dialogue-heavy passages
- dialogue that reads well on the page but would sound unnatural aloud
- exchanges where every character speaks with the same rhythm or vocabulary
- dialogue that could be moved to another scene without changing anything
- dialogue that states context instead of using the context already present
- narration that is semantically correct but rhythmically awkward
- narration whose subject becomes clear too late
- sentences overloaded with modifiers, abstract nouns, or perception filters
- dialogue or narration that preserves semantic intent but still shows the scaffolding of pre-decided meaning
- vocabulary that sounds artificially purified instead of naturally Korean
- dialogue that is short only in a memo-like or label-like way, not in a speakable way
- speaker separation that depends only on word length instead of reaction pattern, register, and emotional behavior
- abstract lines that float above the scene without sensory, relational, or dramatic grounding
