# Horror Starter Sets

Use this file when the user wants a chapter 1 start for horror.

## Audience Tendency Modifiers

### Male-Oriented

- emphasize survival logic, bodily risk, active threat, and decisive movement
- keep fear practical and tied to immediate action

### Female-Oriented

- emphasize violated safety, recognition failure, memory, and relational wrongness
- let dread arrive through intimacy before full danger

### Neutral

- balance atmosphere, checkable anomaly, and readable fear progression
- keep dread concrete without flattening mood

## Quick Variant Blocks

Use these after choosing one of the starter types below.

### Male-Oriented Block

```text
Audience tendency: male-oriented
Emphasis: threat, bodily risk, survival choice
Rule: Keep the opening physically immediate and decision-driven.
```

### Female-Oriented Block

```text
Audience tendency: female-oriented
Emphasis: intimate wrongness, violated trust, emotional dread
Rule: Let fear arrive through relationship, recognition, and uneasy care.
```

### Neutral Block

```text
Audience tendency: neutral
Emphasis: concrete anomaly, atmosphere, broad readability
Rule: Balance dread with scene clarity and forward pull.
```

## Psychological Horror

Core promise:
- something intimate becomes privately wrong before it becomes publicly provable

Best protagonist position:
- a witness who doubts their own reading
- someone emotionally tied to the place or person that now feels wrong

Opening patterns:
- a familiar habit repeats with one unbearable difference
- a loved one says something almost correct, but not quite
- a room looks unchanged until one detail refuses recognition

First chapter checklist:
- one ordinary detail turned unreliable
- one private doubt the protagonist cannot easily say aloud
- one relationship strain caused by that doubt
- one reason the protagonist cannot simply leave

Avoid:
- opening with abstract fear narration only
- proving the horror too early
- making the protagonist instantly certain and eloquent about the wrongness

Micro sample:
```text
엄마는 늘 냉장고 문을 닫고 손등으로 한 번 두드렸다. 별 뜻 없는 버릇이었다. 그런데 그날은 문을 닫은 뒤, 두드리기 전에 내 쪽을 먼저 봤다. 마치 내가 다음 순서를 알고 있는지 확인하듯이.
```

Draft block:
```text
Write the first scene of a Korean psychological horror story.
Audience tendency: [female-oriented / neutral]
Goal: Make one familiar person, object, or routine feel wrong in a way only the protagonist notices at first.
Checklist: Include one intimate detail, one moment of self-doubt, one relational strain, and one reason the protagonist must remain in the scene.
Dialogue rule: Let dialogue sound careful and speakable, as if the speaker fears sounding irrational.
Opening rule: Start after the unease has already begun. Avoid explaining the cause.
```

Revision block:
```text
Revise this chapter opening as Korean psychological horror.
Goal: Increase intimate wrongness without turning the prose abstract.
Rule: Make the fear emerge through routine, misrecognition, and self-doubt. Cut any line that explains the horror more directly than the protagonist would actually think or say.
```

## Ghost Story

Core promise:
- something that should have stayed gone leaves a trace and asks to be noticed

Best protagonist position:
- someone with unfinished connection to the dead, the place, or an old promise
- someone practical enough to resist superstition at first

Opening patterns:
- an object returns to where it used to be
- the same time, sound, or route repeats after years
- an old name appears where no one should know it

First chapter checklist:
- one returning trace
- one link to the past
- one taboo or family silence
- one immediate practical reaction

Avoid:
- explaining the ghost's whole history in the opener
- using vague atmosphere without a concrete returning sign
- making everyone instantly believe

Micro sample:
```text
비어 있는 줄 알았던 신발장 맨 아래 칸에 젖은 아이 운동화가 가지런히 놓여 있었다. 열세 해 전, 강가에서 끝내 찾지 못했던 것과 같은 색이었다.
```

Draft block:
```text
Write the first scene of a Korean ghost story.
Audience tendency: [neutral / female-oriented]
Goal: Hook with one returning trace tied to place memory, taboo, or unresolved grief.
Checklist: Include one impossible return, one past connection, one practical response, and one unanswered question.
Dialogue rule: Keep dialogue brief, wary, and slightly taboo-conscious.
Opening rule: Let the trace arrive before the backstory.
```

Revision block:
```text
Revise this chapter opening as a Korean ghost story.
Goal: Strengthen recurrence, memory, and the feeling that the place remembers.
Rule: Keep one concrete returning sign at the center. Cut any generic spooky wording that is not attached to an object, place, or repeated action.
```

## Creature Horror

Core promise:
- the threat is embodied, close, and forces survival decisions now

Best protagonist position:
- someone who can act under pressure but lacks full information
- a group member who must choose between speed, rescue, and risk

Opening patterns:
- pursuit already underway
- a contaminated wound or trace is noticed too late
- a barricade, door, or vehicle fails under pressure

First chapter checklist:
- one bodily risk
- one obstacle or failing barrier
- one immediate choice
- one sign that the threat is learning, spreading, or closing in

Avoid:
- stopping the motion for long explanation
- describing the creature in full before fear has landed
- dialogue that sounds too complete for running, hiding, or shouting

Micro sample:
```text
지훈은 철문을 끝까지 당겼는데도 아래가 닫히지 않는다는 걸 두 번째 손잡이를 잡고서야 알았다. 문틈 사이로 검은 물이 아니라, 손톱 같은 게 먼저 밀려 들어왔다.
```

Draft block:
```text
Write the first scene of a Korean creature horror story.
Audience tendency: [male-oriented / neutral]
Goal: Start with immediate bodily risk and force a survival decision within the first lines.
Checklist: Include one barrier failure, one physical threat cue, one urgent choice, and one sign the threat is still approaching.
Dialogue rule: Keep dialogue short enough to shout, warn, or refuse under motion.
Opening rule: Enter after the danger has already started.
```

Revision block:
```text
Revise this chapter opening as Korean creature horror.
Goal: Increase physical urgency and embodied threat.
Rule: Cut decorative fear language. Make sound, motion, distance, injury risk, and obstacles do the work.
```
