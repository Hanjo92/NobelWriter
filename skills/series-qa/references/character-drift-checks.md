# Character Drift Checks

Use this file when the main risk is that characters no longer behave like the same people across chapters, arcs, or emotional turning points.

## Core Principle

Character drift is not just "the character feels off." It is usually one of these failures:

- motivation changes without enough cause
- relational change does not leave residue
- consequences do not alter later behavior
- the protagonist loses agency and becomes plot-reactive
- secondary characters flatten into delivery devices

## Common Drift Modes

### Motivation Reset

Symptoms:

- a character's urgent desire weakens or vanishes between arcs
- major sacrifices stop affecting what they pursue next
- choices no longer line up with the pressure established earlier

Likely cause:

- the story engine moved, but the character engine was not updated
- new plot needs overrode prior motivation without transitional cost

### Relationship Residue Loss

Symptoms:

- confession, betrayal, rescue, humiliation, or reconciliation happens, but later scenes behave as if the event barely mattered
- trust and distance return to baseline too quickly

Likely cause:

- the manuscript preserves old dynamics for convenience
- no behavioral carryover was defined after the turning point

### Agency Collapse

Symptoms:

- the protagonist increasingly waits for reveals, attacks, or invitations
- decisions happen around them more than because of them
- scenes begin with the character reacting to incoming plot instead of making moves

Likely cause:

- the story is conserving mystery or escalation by withholding initiative
- the protagonist's desire is no longer specific enough to generate action

### Emotional Consequence Fade

Symptoms:

- grief, fear, anger, shame, or relief appears vividly in one chapter and barely affects later behavior
- emotional fallout is named once and then operationally forgotten

Likely cause:

- consequence is being staged for scene intensity, not for long-form continuity

### Functional Side Character Flattening

Symptoms:

- side characters mainly explain, warn, deliver clues, or trigger plot beats
- their earlier contradictions, wants, or loyalties stop shaping scenes

Likely cause:

- utility has replaced character pressure in scene design

## QA Questions

- What does this character want now, and is that still consistent with the prior range
- What event should still be affecting their behavior
- What has changed in how they trust, avoid, risk, or speak
- Are they initiating pressure or mostly receiving it
- If this side character were removed, would the same function be delivered by anyone

## Evidence Standard

When reporting drift, name:

- the last chapter where the character still felt stable
- the first chapter where the drift becomes visible
- the turning point that should have changed them
- the missing residue in later behavior

Do not call it drift unless you can show the prior baseline.

## Repair Rule

Fix drift by restoring behavioral carryover, not by adding explanatory dialogue after the fact. Prefer:

1. changed decision patterns
2. altered trust or avoidance behavior
3. visible cost in relationships, status, or self-control
4. scene-entry goals that reflect the current internal state

## Recheck Questions

- Does the character now make choices that follow from the flagged turning point
- Does later behavior preserve at least one lasting effect of the event
- Has the protagonist regained initiative in the sampled range
- Do side characters now create scene pressure instead of only serving plot logistics
