# Sample Progression Fantasy QA Report

Use this file when you want a completed example focused on growth, training, and power-system momentum.

## Example

```text
Audit scope:
- Manuscript range: Episodes 41-55
- Planning documents checked: rank-up outline, power system notes, training arc card set
- Audit mode: arc checkpoint
- Sampling limit: full read of Episodes 41-55, spot check of Episodes 37-40 for pre-arc stakes

Complaint under test:
- Reported symptom: the protagonist is getting stronger, but the recent growth feels less exciting
- Working hypothesis: advancement is occurring without enough cost or qualitative challenge change

Scope note:
- What was checked: breakthrough beats, training loop variation, post-upgrade challenge texture, cost carryover
- What was not checked: late-season cosmology expansion, prose-level action clarity
- Confidence limit: high confidence for the sampled training arc, medium confidence for larger system inflation outside the range

Confirmed findings:
1. Rank-up gains are not changing challenge type
   Severity: High
   Confidence: High
   Root cause or symptom: Root cause
   First break point: Episode 45
   Evidence: After the mid-arc breakthrough, the protagonist keeps facing stronger versions of the same combat problem rather than needing new tactics, tradeoffs, or strategic thinking.
   Expected function: Progression should unlock qualitatively different obstacles, not only higher numbers.
   Observed failure: Advancement increases scale without refreshing the dramatic shape of challenge.
   Reader-visible damage: Power growth feels measurable but less thrilling than earlier breakthroughs.
   Smallest viable fix: Introduce one post-rank-up obstacle that current stats cannot solve without a new cost, method, or sacrifice.
   Recheck condition: The next major confrontation after the breakthrough must require a different problem-solving mode than the pre-breakthrough fights.

2. Training loop repetition is dulling the arc
   Severity: High
   Confidence: High
   Root cause or symptom: Root cause
   First break point: Episode 43
   Evidence: Episodes 43, 46, and 49 repeat a similar train-fail-adjust-improve sequence with only superficial changes in terminology and environment.
   Expected function: Each training stage should alter stakes, method, or consequence.
   Observed failure: The loop repeats its dramatic function without enough mutation.
   Reader-visible damage: Episodes blur together and growth starts to feel routine instead of hard-won.
   Smallest viable fix: Replace one loop segment with a cost-heavy setback that affects status, body, or team trust.
   Recheck condition: Revised training episodes must differ in dramatic function, not only in technique label.

3. Breakthrough cost is fading too quickly
   Severity: Medium
   Confidence: Medium
   Root cause or symptom: Downstream symptom
   First break point: Episode 47
   Evidence: The breakthrough initially imposes bodily strain and resource drain, but those burdens stop constraining action within two episodes.
   Expected function: A major gain should carry visible price long enough to shape subsequent choices.
   Observed failure: Cost exists for flavor and then evaporates.
   Reader-visible damage: Readers register the system as generous rather than demanding.
   Smallest viable fix: Extend the breakthrough cost into later tactical limits or relational strain.
   Recheck condition: The next two post-breakthrough episodes must show the gain's cost affecting at least one important decision.

Hypotheses needing broader review:
- The system may be expanding too fast overall, but the sampled arc is not wide enough to prove broader readability collapse.

What is still working:
- The rival track still provides strong comparative pressure.
- The power system remains understandable at the local level.
- Episode 52 restores momentum once the breakthrough is tied to team risk.

Revision priority:
1. Make post-rank-up challenges qualitatively different.
2. Break the repeated training loop with a meaningful setback.
3. Preserve breakthrough cost across subsequent episodes.
```

## Why This Example Works

- It separates visible growth from satisfying growth.
- It checks whether training arcs mutate in dramatic function.
- It treats cost carryover as essential to progression credibility.
