# Sample Romance QA Report

Use this file when you want a completed example focused on relationship-driven fiction.

## Example

```text
Audit scope:
- Manuscript range: Chapters 22-34
- Planning documents checked: romance beat outline, chapter cards
- Audit mode: arc checkpoint
- Sampling limit: full read of Chapters 22-34, spot check of Chapters 18-21 for the prior breakthrough

Complaint under test:
- Reported symptom: the main pair still has chemistry, but the relationship feels stuck
- Working hypothesis: repeated attraction beats are replacing relational change

Scope note:
- What was checked: pair scenes, confession delays, aftermath behavior, chapter-end emotional state shifts
- What was not checked: side-couple subplot, line-level dialogue polish
- Confidence limit: high confidence for the sampled pair arc, medium confidence for the earliest onset before Chapter 22

Confirmed findings:
1. The pair is circling the same near-confession beat without new emotional price
   Severity: High
   Confidence: High
   Classification: Root cause
   First break point: Chapter 24
   Evidence: Chapters 24, 27, and 30 each stage an almost-confession followed by withdrawal, but the withdrawals do not cost trust, status, or opportunity in a way that changes the next interaction.
   Expected function: Repeated delay beats should either deepen vulnerability or alter the relationship state.
   Observed failure: Delay is preserving tension while freezing actual movement.
   Reader-visible damage: The pair remains readable and attractive, but the romance feels stalled rather than charged.
   Smallest viable fix: Turn one near-confession withdrawal into a scene with lasting relational consequence such as lowered trust, changed access, or visible jealousy cost.
   Recheck condition: The next major hesitation beat must leave the pair in a measurably different relationship state.

2. Emotional risk is unevenly distributed
   Severity: Medium
   Confidence: High
   Classification: Root cause
   First break point: Chapter 25
   Evidence: One lead repeatedly admits fear, asks for clarification, and changes behavior, while the other retains mystery without paying comparable emotional cost.
   Expected function: Both sides of the central pair should carry visible romantic risk.
   Observed failure: Vulnerability is concentrated on one side, making the relationship dynamic feel imbalanced.
   Reader-visible damage: The romance starts to read as pursuit plus withholding rather than mutual escalation.
   Smallest viable fix: Add one scene where the emotionally safer lead must risk status, pride, or attachment security.
   Recheck condition: Within the next three pair scenes, both leads must take a visible relational risk.

3. The breakthrough at Chapter 21 is not being carried forward
   Severity: Medium
   Confidence: Medium
   Classification: Downstream symptom
   First break point: Chapter 22
   Evidence: The late-Chapter 21 intimacy shift is treated as meaningful in the moment, but Chapters 22-26 revert to pre-breakthrough caution patterns with little behavioral residue.
   Expected function: A breakthrough should change later address, access, jealousy logic, or emotional threshold.
   Observed failure: The story restores baseline tension too quickly.
   Reader-visible damage: Readers feel that important scenes are being emotionally discounted after the fact.
   Smallest viable fix: Carry the Chapter 21 shift into touch tolerance, speech boldness, or visible discomfort in public scenes.
   Recheck condition: The next post-breakthrough sequence must preserve at least one changed behavior from Chapter 21.

Hypotheses needing broader review:
- The imbalance in emotional risk may originate in the character setup itself, but the sampled range is not sufficient to prove that root design issue.

What is still working:
- Banter remains lively and specific to the pair.
- Secondary interference raises pressure cleanly without fully hijacking the romance.
- Chapter 33 briefly restores momentum by attaching jealousy to a social cost.

Revision priority:
1. Break the repeated near-confession loop with lasting consequence.
2. Rebalance emotional risk across the pair.
3. Preserve the Chapter 21 breakthrough in later behavior.
```

## Why This Example Works

- It distinguishes chemistry from actual relationship movement.
- It treats delay as a structural device that must earn its place.
- It checks whether a romantic breakthrough leaves residue instead of vanishing.
